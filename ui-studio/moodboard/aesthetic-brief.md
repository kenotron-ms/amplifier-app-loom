# Aesthetic Brief — Loom Types Icon Research

> Generated from 10 references. Review and edit before using in /storyboard.

## Context
This moodboard is visual research for a **macOS system tray icon** (22×22px primary size) for **Amplifier Loom** — an app that weaves bundles into dev sessions. The references are drawn from traditional and mechanical looms of multiple types, with special focus on structural geometry that survives extreme size reduction.

## Palette
Extracted colors: `#2A1C14` `#8B6349` `#D4A060` `#E8E3D9` `#4B7178` `#9A9490` `#8A3A3A`

- **Background:** light/warm neutral — `#F2EDE4` (parchment/linen)
- **Primary dark:** `#2A1C14` — near-black with warm undertone, like oiled wood grain
- **Wood brown:** `#8B6349` — the natural timber of loom frames, warm and structural
- **Amber thread:** `#D4A060` — the color of tensioned warp thread, golden and alive
- **Parchment:** `#E8E3D9` — bleached cotton, natural linen, unbleached warp
- **Steel teal:** `#4B7178` — the cool counterpoint — heddle eyes, worn metal, the machine in the craft
- **Iron gray:** `#9A9490` — the neutral metalwork, heddle wire, structural steel
- **Rust terracotta:** `#8A3A3A` — punch card wear marks, aged textile, terracotta ceramic shuttles

The palette is emphatically **warm-neutral** with one intentional cool accent (the steel teal). This tension — warm organic wood versus cool precise metal — is the central visual character of the loom.

## Typography
- **Style:** Geometric sans or technical monospaced — precision over warmth
- **Weight character:** Medium/regular — not decorative, not heavy. Functional.
- **Density:** Sparse and architectural — labels in technical drawings, not editorial
- **Character:** Engineering drawings use tightly spaced uppercase labels with wide tracking. That register.

---

## Composition Character
- **Spatial rhythm:** Measured — not cramped. Threads are always evenly spaced.
- **Structural tendency:** Deeply **geometric** — parallel lines, right angles, grids, and the singular diagonal of the interlacing thread
- **Visual weight:** Balanced with one focal tension point — the **crossing/intersection** is always the visual anchor
- **Key structural primitives observed across all loom types:**
  - Parallel verticals (warp threads under tension)
  - Single orthogonal crossing (the weft insertion)
  - The frame: a rectangle that holds tension
  - The hole/eye: the point a thread passes *through* a rigid surface

## Reference Summary

The collection consistently returns to a single structural truth: **two sets of parallel lines crossing at 90°**, with the crossing point being the locus of all action. Whether it's the Jacquard's punch-card binary grid, the heddle's rows of metal eyes, the inkle loom's geometric peg routing, or the plain-weave over-under diagram — the intersection is the thing.

The references point toward a mark that is fundamentally **two bars, one over the other**, with the crossing point as the focal element. The mechanical precision of the Jacquard (binary holes, perfect rows) and the tensioned parallel geometry of the warp threads are the most transferable concepts to a 22px icon. Organic warmth (wood, texture) is the material feeling but cannot survive size reduction; the **structural abstraction** (crossing, grid, hole) is what remains legible at small size.

---

## Anti-Slop Notes

- **Do not use a weaving texture/pattern as the icon background** — textile weave patterns at small sizes become visual noise, not signal. The mark needs hard geometric clarity.
- **Avoid Celtic knotwork or ornamental interlace** — those forms are too complex to read at 22px and skew toward decorative rather than functional tool iconography.
- **The "L" letter is a loom but is corner-heavy** — the research confirms this. The structural visual language of looms uses centered crossing and parallel tension, not corner-anchored letterforms.
- **Warm wood tones are contextual, not icon colors** — for the icon itself, warm wood will look muddy in macOS tray context (dark menubar). The steel teal (#4B7178) is the single color most likely to read cleanly in both light/dark menu bar modes.
- **Do not round the crossing** — every reference shows the loom intersection as a hard 90° right angle. Rounded corners on the icon mark would contradict the entire structural language of the form.
- **The hole/eye is as important as the bar** — heddle eyes (the holes threads pass through) are one of the most recurring structural elements. A mark that shows a line *passing through* something may be more evocative than two bars merely crossing.

## Icon Concept Candidates (Distilled from References)

Based on this research, the strongest concepts for the 22×22px mark:

1. **Two crossing bars, hard 90°, one over** — the absolute simplest abstraction of the over-under weave. Two rectangles, centered, one clearly on top. Works in 2 colors (indigo + teal from prior explorations) or monochrome.

2. **Tension grid: three parallel verticals bisected by one horizontal** — like the warp threads on a rigid heddle. Three (or five) vertical lines, one horizontal crossing all of them. Immediately legible as "weaving" structure.

3. **The heddle eye: a bar with a hole** — a single horizontal bar (or two) with a circular cutout centered on it. The thread passes *through* the hole. This is the most distinctive structural form in the loom vocabulary and doesn't appear in any other tool category.

4. **Jacquard grid: 2×3 or 3×3 filled/empty squares** — binary pixel art derived from punch-card holes. Would read as "programmatic" as well as "woven" — appropriate for a developer tool.
